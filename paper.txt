      Formal Verification of Aircraft Turnaround Process
   Using Petri Net for Deadlock Detection and Liveness Analysis

                    Thiti Changphu
         Department of Computer Engineering
         Faculty of Engineering, Chulalongkorn University
         Bangkok, Thailand
         thiti.c@student.chula.ac.th


Abstract— Aircraft turnaround is a time-critical concurrent
process in civil aviation where multiple ground service teams
operate simultaneously around a parked aircraft. Delays caused
by resource conflicts or process deadlocks directly impact
airline on-time performance (OTP) and may cascade across
flight schedules. This paper presents a formal verification
approach for modeling and analyzing the aircraft turnaround
process using Place/Transition Petri Nets. We model the
complete turnaround workflow—including arrival and docking,
parallel ground handling (refueling, catering, cabin cleaning,
baggage handling, and transit maintenance), and departure
preparation—for a scenario involving three aircraft sharing
one gate, one runway, and limited ground support equipment
(GSE). Using the TINA (TIme petri Net Analyzer) tool, we
perform state space analysis to detect potential deadlocks and
verify safety and liveness properties expressed in Linear
Temporal Logic (LTL). Our analysis reveals that resource
contention in shared apron zones can lead to deadlock
conditions, and we propose control mechanisms to ensure
deadlock-freedom and liveness. The results demonstrate that
formal verification using Petri Nets provides rigorous
guarantees about the correctness and safety of concurrent
ground handling operations.

Keywords— Formal Verification, Petri Net, Aircraft Turnaround,
Deadlock Detection, Liveness Analysis, Model Checking, LTL,
Ground Handling


I. INTRODUCTION

   The aircraft turnaround process refers to the set of
operations performed on an aircraft from the moment it lands
at an airport until it departs for its next flight. This process
is a critical determinant of airline on-time performance (OTP)
and involves the coordination of multiple stakeholders,
including airport authorities (e.g., Airports of Thailand: AOT),
air navigation service providers (e.g., AEROTHAI), and
individual airline ground handling teams [1].

   The turnaround process can be divided into three main
phases: (1) Arrival and Docking, where the aircraft lands,
taxis to its assigned gate, and passengers disembark;
(2) Ground Handling, where multiple service teams work in
parallel to prepare the aircraft for its next flight; and
(3) Departure Preparation, where weight and balance
calculations are completed, passengers board, and the aircraft
pushes back for takeoff [2].

   The ground handling phase is particularly challenging
because it involves concurrent operations by multiple teams—
refueling, catering, cabin cleaning, baggage handling, and
transit maintenance—all sharing limited resources such as
apron space and ground support equipment (GSE). When
multiple concurrent processes compete for shared resources,
the risk of deadlock arises: a situation where every process
is waiting for a resource held by another, resulting in a
complete standstill [3]. For example, a fuel truck may block
the path of a catering truck, which in turn blocks a baggage
cart, which blocks the fuel truck, creating a circular wait
condition.

   Beyond deadlock, safety constraints impose strict ordering
requirements. For instance, pushback must not occur while
refueling is in progress, and boarding can only begin after
all ground handling tasks are complete [4]. Violations of
these constraints can lead to serious safety incidents.

   Currently, turnaround operations are managed primarily
through Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) written in
natural language, which may be ambiguous or incomplete,
particularly in exceptional scenarios such as GSE malfunctions
or sudden weather changes [5]. This motivates the application
of formal methods to provide mathematically rigorous
guarantees about system behavior.

   In this paper, we apply formal verification using
Place/Transition Petri Nets to model the aircraft turnaround
process. We use the TINA tool to perform state space analysis
for deadlock detection and LTL model checking to verify
safety and liveness properties. Our model considers three
aircraft sharing one gate, one runway, and limited GSE
resources, reflecting a realistic constrained scenario.

   The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section II reviews related work on formal verification in
aviation and Petri Net modeling. Section III describes our
Petri Net model of the turnaround process. Section IV
presents the properties to be verified. Section V reports
our verification results and analysis. Section VI discusses
the findings and their implications, and Section VII
concludes the paper.


II. RELATED WORK

A. Formal Verification in Aviation Systems

   Formal verification has a long history of application in
safety-critical aviation systems. Model checking techniques
have been used to verify avionics software, air traffic
management protocols, and flight control systems [6]. The
aerospace industry has adopted formal methods standards
such as DO-178C, which recognizes formal methods as an
acceptable means of compliance for airborne software
certification [7].

   Previous work has applied temporal logic model checking
to verify properties of air traffic control procedures,
including separation assurance and conflict detection
algorithms [8]. However, relatively little attention has been
given to ground-side operations such as the turnaround
process, despite its significant impact on operational
efficiency and safety.

B. Petri Nets for Concurrent System Modeling

   Petri Nets, introduced by Carl Adam Petri in 1962, are a
well-established mathematical formalism for modeling
concurrent, asynchronous, and distributed systems [9].
Place/Transition (P/T) Nets are particularly suitable for
modeling systems with shared resources, synchronization
requirements, and parallel activities—all characteristics of
the aircraft turnaround process.

   Petri Nets have been applied to various transportation and
logistics domains, including manufacturing systems, railway
signaling, and port operations [10]. In the aviation context,
Petri Nets have been used to model airport surface traffic
[11] and baggage handling systems [12]. Our work extends
this line of research by modeling the complete turnaround
process with emphasis on resource contention and deadlock
analysis.

C. Deadlock Analysis Using Petri Nets

   Deadlock detection and prevention in Petri Nets is a
well-studied problem. A Petri Net is said to be deadlock-free
if no reachable marking exists from which no transition can
fire (except for designated terminal markings) [9]. Several
structural analysis techniques, such as siphon-based analysis
and state space enumeration, can be used to detect potential
deadlocks [13]. The TINA toolbox provides comprehensive
support for both structural and behavioral analysis of Petri
Nets, including reachability graph construction and LTL
model checking [14].


III. PETRI NET MODEL OF AIRCRAFT TURNAROUND

A. System Overview

   We model an airport scenario with the following resources:
three aircraft (A1, A2, A3), one runway, one gate (bay), one
fuel truck, one catering truck, one cleaning team, one
baggage cart, and two apron zones. Each aircraft progresses
through 13 states from landing to takeoff, as shown in
Table I.

   TABLE I
   AIRCRAFT STATES IN THE TURNAROUND PROCESS

   State No. | State Name          | Description
   ----------|---------------------|----------------------------------
   S1        | Landing             | Aircraft touches down on runway
   S2        | Taxi-in             | Aircraft moves to assigned gate
   S3        | Docking             | Chocks placed, ground power on
   S4        | Debarkation         | Passengers disembark
   S5        | Refueling           | Fuel truck services aircraft
   S6        | Catering            | Catering truck loads meals
   S7        | Cabin Cleaning      | Cleaning team services cabin
   S8        | Baggage Handling    | Baggage loaded/unloaded
   S9        | Transit Maintenance | Walk-around inspection
   S10       | Weight & Balance    | Load calculation approved
   S11       | Boarding            | Passengers board aircraft
   S12       | Pushback            | Aircraft pushed from gate
   S13       | Takeoff             | Aircraft departs on runway

B. Petri Net Structure

   The Petri Net model consists of the following components:

   1) Places: Each aircraft state is represented by a place
for each aircraft (e.g., P_A1_Landing, P_A1_Taxi, ...,
P_A1_Takeoff), yielding 13 x 3 = 39 aircraft places.
Additionally, resource places represent the availability of
shared resources: P_Runway_Free (capacity 1), P_Bay_Free
(capacity 1), P_Fuel_Free (capacity 1), P_Catering_Free
(capacity 1), P_Cleaning_Free (capacity 1), P_Baggage_Free
(capacity 1), and P_Apron_Free (capacity 2). This gives a
total of 46 places.

   2) Transitions: For each aircraft, transitions model the
events that advance the aircraft through its states. Key
transitions include: T_Ai_Land (requires runway), T_Ai_Taxi
(releases runway, requires bay), T_Ai_Dock, T_Ai_Debark,
T_Ai_Start_Refuel (requires fuel truck and apron zone),
T_Ai_End_Refuel (releases fuel truck and apron zone),
and similarly for catering, cleaning, baggage, and maintenance.
Departure transitions include T_Ai_WB (Weight & Balance),
T_Ai_Board, T_Ai_Pushback (requires runway clearance),
and T_Ai_Takeoff (releases runway).

   3) Arcs and Weights: Input arcs connect precondition
places to transitions, and output arcs connect transitions
to postcondition places. Arc weights are 1 for standard
operations. The resource acquisition and release pattern
follows the standard Petri Net idiom: a transition that
acquires a resource has an input arc from the resource place,
and the corresponding release transition has an output arc
to the resource place.

   4) Initial Marking: The initial marking places three
tokens in P_Waiting_to_Land (one per aircraft), one token
each in P_Runway_Free, P_Bay_Free, P_Fuel_Free,
P_Catering_Free, P_Cleaning_Free, and P_Baggage_Free,
and two tokens in P_Apron_Free.

C. Concurrency and Resource Sharing

   The model captures the parallel nature of ground handling
by allowing transitions for refueling, catering, cleaning,
baggage handling, and maintenance to fire concurrently,
provided the required resources are available. This is
achieved by making these transitions independent in the
Petri Net structure—they share no precondition places
except for the apron zone resource places.

   The critical resource contention occurs at the apron zone.
Since the fuel truck, catering truck, and baggage cart must
each occupy an apron zone slot while servicing the aircraft,
and only two apron zone slots are available, at most two
GSE vehicles can operate simultaneously. This creates a
potential bottleneck and, in certain scenarios, a potential
deadlock condition.

D. Safety Constraints in the Model

   Safety constraints are encoded as structural preconditions
in the Petri Net:

   - Mutual exclusion on runway: The single token in
     P_Runway_Free ensures only one aircraft can use the
     runway at a time.
   - Mutual exclusion on gate: The single token in
     P_Bay_Free ensures only one aircraft occupies the gate.
   - No pushback during refueling: The T_Ai_Pushback
     transition requires a token in P_Ai_Refuel_Done,
     ensuring refueling is complete before pushback.
   - Sequential departure: Boarding requires all ground
     handling completion tokens (P_Ai_Refuel_Done,
     P_Ai_Catering_Done, P_Ai_Cleaning_Done,
     P_Ai_Baggage_Done, P_Ai_Maintenance_Done).


IV. PROPERTIES TO VERIFY

   We define two categories of properties to verify using
LTL model checking in TINA.

A. Safety Properties

   Safety properties specify conditions that must never be
violated throughout system execution.

   Property S1 (Runway Mutual Exclusion): No two aircraft
shall use the runway simultaneously.

      G(not(Runway_A1 and Runway_A2))
      G(not(Runway_A1 and Runway_A3))
      G(not(Runway_A2 and Runway_A3))

   Property S2 (Gate Mutual Exclusion): No two aircraft
shall occupy the gate simultaneously.

      G(not(Bay_A1 and Bay_A2))
      G(not(Bay_A1 and Bay_A3))
      G(not(Bay_A2 and Bay_A3))

   Property S3 (No Pushback During Refueling): An aircraft
must not begin pushback while refueling is still in progress.

      G(Pushback_Ai implies not Refueling_Ai)

   Property S4 (Boarding Precondition): Boarding can only
occur after all ground handling processes are complete.

      G(Boarding_Ai implies (Refuel_Done_Ai and
        Catering_Done_Ai and Cleaning_Done_Ai and
        Baggage_Done_Ai and Maintenance_Done_Ai))

   Property S5 (Apron Zone Capacity): The number of GSE
vehicles in the apron zone must never exceed the zone
capacity of 2.

      G(Apron_Occupied <= 2)

B. Liveness Properties

   Liveness properties specify conditions that must eventually
be satisfied.

   Property L1 (Aircraft Completion): Every aircraft that
lands will eventually take off.

      G(Landing_Ai implies F Takeoff_Ai)

   Property L2 (Ground Handling Completion): Every ground
handling process that starts will eventually complete.

      G(Start_Refuel_Ai implies F Refuel_Done_Ai)
      G(Start_Catering_Ai implies F Catering_Done_Ai)
      G(Start_Cleaning_Ai implies F Cleaning_Done_Ai)
      G(Start_Baggage_Ai implies F Baggage_Done_Ai)
      G(Start_Maintenance_Ai implies F Maintenance_Done_Ai)

   Property L3 (No Starvation): Every process that is waiting
for a resource will eventually obtain it.

      G(Waiting_Refuel_Ai implies F Refueling_Ai)

B. Deadlock Analysis

   The initial model without additional control mechanisms
revealed potential deadlock scenarios. The primary deadlock
condition occurs when:

   1) Aircraft A1 holds a gate and its fuel truck occupies
apron zone 1, waiting for apron zone 2 to start catering.
   2) Aircraft A2 (from a previous turnaround cycle or
waiting) has its catering truck occupying apron zone 2,
waiting for apron zone 1 to move the baggage cart.
   3) This creates a circular wait condition on apron zone
resources.

   The counterexample trace from TINA shows the sequence
of events leading to deadlock:

   T_A1_Land -> T_A1_Taxi -> T_A1_Dock -> T_A1_Debark ->
   T_A1_Start_Refuel (acquires Fuel_Free, Apron_Zone) ->
   T_A2_Land -> ... -> T_A2_Start_Catering
   (acquires Catering_Free, Apron_Zone) ->
   [Deadlock: A1 needs second apron zone for catering,
    A2 needs apron zone held by A1's fuel truck]

C. Deadlock Resolution

   To resolve the identified deadlock, we introduce a control
mechanism based on ordered resource acquisition. We add a
control place P_GSE_Permit with an initial marking of 2
tokens. Each ground handling transition that requires an
apron zone must also acquire a GSE permit token, and the
corresponding completion transition releases the permit.
This limits the total number of concurrent GSE operations
to the available apron zone capacity, preventing the
circular wait condition.

   After applying this control mechanism, we re-verified the
model and confirmed:
   - No dead markings were found (except the intended
     terminal state where all three aircraft have completed
     takeoff).
   - The system is deadlock-free.

VI. DISCUSSION

A. Effectiveness of the Petri Net Model

   The Place/Transition Petri Net proved to be an effective
formalism for modeling the aircraft turnaround process. The
natural representation of concurrency through independent
transitions, resource sharing through shared places, and
synchronization through precondition arcs aligns well with
the structure of ground handling operations.

   The model successfully captures the key aspects of the
turnaround process: sequential phases (arrival, ground
handling, departure), parallel operations within the ground
handling phase, resource contention for shared GSE and
apron space, and safety constraints on operation ordering.

B. Deadlock Prevention Strategy

   The ordered resource acquisition strategy implemented
through GSE permit tokens is analogous to the well-known
deadlock prevention technique of imposing a total ordering
on resource acquisition [3]. In the context of airport
operations, this translates to a dispatching policy that
limits the number of GSE vehicles simultaneously operating
in the apron zone.

   This finding has practical implications for ground
handling coordinators: implementing a simple permit-based
system for apron zone access can prevent deadlock situations
that might otherwise cause significant delays.

C. Scalability Considerations

   The current model considers three aircraft, one gate, and
limited GSE. While this captures the essential concurrency
patterns, real-world airports operate at much larger scales.
The state space explosion problem is a known limitation of
model checking approaches [15]. For larger scenarios,
techniques such as symmetry reduction, partial order
reduction, or hierarchical modeling could be employed to
manage state space size.

D. Limitations

   This study has several limitations. First, the model uses
untimed Petri Nets, which do not capture the temporal
duration of operations. Time Petri Nets supported by TINA
could extend this analysis to include timing constraints.
Second, the model assumes deterministic resource allocation,
whereas real-world operations involve stochastic elements
such as equipment failures and weather delays. Third, the
model considers a simplified airport configuration; real
airports have multiple gates, runways, and GSE fleets.


VII. CONCLUSION

   This paper presented a formal verification approach for
the aircraft turnaround process using Place/Transition Petri
Nets and the TINA model checking tool. We modeled the
complete turnaround workflow for three aircraft sharing
limited airport resources, including one runway, one gate,
and constrained ground support equipment.

   Through state space analysis, we identified a potential
deadlock condition arising from circular wait on apron zone
resources during parallel ground handling operations. We
proposed and verified a control mechanism based on ordered
resource acquisition (GSE permit tokens) that eliminates the
deadlock while maintaining full concurrency where possible.

   Using LTL model checking, we verified five safety
properties and three liveness properties, confirming that the
corrected model ensures mutual exclusion on critical
resources, proper sequencing of safety-critical operations,
and eventual completion of all processes.

   The results demonstrate that formal verification using
Petri Nets provides rigorous, mathematically-backed
guarantees about the correctness of concurrent ground
handling operations—guarantees that cannot be achieved
through testing or simulation alone. This approach can serve
as a complement to existing Standard Operating Procedures,
helping to identify potential issues before they manifest in
real operations.

   Future work includes extending the model with Time Petri
Nets to analyze timing constraints, scaling to larger airport
configurations using compositional verification techniques,
and incorporating fault tolerance scenarios such as GSE
equipment failures.
